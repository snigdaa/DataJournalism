Correlations Discovered Between Health Risks and Age, Income

There seems to be a slight but not tight correlation Between poverty and the lack of healthcare; there seems to be a similar loose correlation between the poverty level and percent of people who smoke, but most states stay within a median range. Obesity across the states doesn't seem to have much of a connection with poverty level, although some states with higher poverty levels also have a very high obesity level. This could have something to do with fast food/unhealthy food being more affordable than having to buy and make healthier food, or buy

The median age for most states is common, between 30 and 40. There seems to be absolutely no correlation between median age and lack of healthcare, as the states span randomly across the healthcare axis when compared to age. Similarly, most states stay within a common amount of smoking, between 15 and 25% for most states. And again with obesity, there seems to be no direct correlation between median age and obesity levels. We can conclude that the median age of each state does not much affect the health risks of the state.

There seems to be a general downward trend when comparing income and lack of healthcare, which makes sense -- more wealthy states have better access to healthcare. There also seems to be an interesting and even stronger relationship between smoking and income. Poorer states have much higher smoking percentages than wealthier states, which could have to do with the environmental influences of poorer states as well as the affordability of cigarettes vs. other drugs. Obesity in poorer states is also much more prevalent than in wealthier states, which could again be because, as we mentioned in the poverty analysis, healthier food, especially when compared to junk and fast food, can be much more expensive to buy or make, and people with lower incomes may not be able to afford it.
